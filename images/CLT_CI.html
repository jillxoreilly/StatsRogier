      Estimating the sampling distribution from a sample¶

      The Central Limit Theorem tells us that (for large 𝑛n) the sampling distribution of the mean is (𝜇,𝜎𝑛√)N(μ,σn).

      If we know the parent distribution (as we did for the UK Brexdex distribution), then we know 𝜇μ and 𝜎σ and can calculate the sampling distribution of the mean for samples of size 𝑛n - or indeed we can simulate the sampling distribution as we did above.

      However, a more common situation is that we just have one sample (of size 𝑛n). Then we do not know the parameters 𝜇μ and 𝜎σ of the parent population.

      Nonetheless we want to estimate what the random variation in sample means would be if we were to draw lots more samples of size 𝑛n from the same parent population, as this allows us to establish a plausible range in which the true parameters 𝜇μ and 𝜎σ might fall.

      The Central Limit Theorem allows us to do this by the following logic:

      (For large 𝑛n)

      -- The true sampling distribution of the mean is (𝜇,𝜎𝑛√)N(μ,σn)

      -- Our best estimate of the sampling distribution of the mean is therefore (𝑥¯,𝑠𝑛√)N(x¯,sn), where 𝑥¯x¯ and 𝑠s are our estimates of the mean and standard deviation of the parent distibution, based on the sample

      ---- 𝑥¯x¯ is the mean of the sample, ie sample.mean()

      ---- 𝑠s is the standard deviation of the sample, ie sample.std()

      Confidence intervals¶

      A 95% confidence interval is an interval around a sample mean with a width such that

      • if we drew many independent samples (all of size n) from the parent distribution
      • ... and we drew an interval of that width around each sample mean
      • ... then 95% of those intervals would contain the 𝜇μ, the true mean of the parent population

      Another way of thinking about this is that for a single 95% confidence interval computed on a single sample, we (the researcher) have 95% confidence that that interval contains 𝜇μ, the true mean of the parent population

      The magical 𝑍=1.96Z=1.96¶

      For a normally distributed variable:

      • 95% of cases fall within 1.96 standard deviations of the mean
      • 99% of cases fall within 2.33 standard deviations of the mean

      For example, say women's heights in the UK follow a normal distribution (164.3,6.1)N(164.3,6.1)

      We expect 95% of women to have a height between 164.3−1.96×(6.1)164.3−1.96×(6.1), and 164.3+1.96×(6.1)164.3+1.96×(6.1), ie between 152.3 cm and 176.3 cm

      95% CI for maths IQ¶

      The sampling distribution of the mean for the maths IQ data, with samples of size 60, was estimated to be (𝑥¯,𝑠𝑛√)N(x¯,sn) = (102.75,1.537043)N(102.75,1.537043).

      Our 95% CI for the mean of the parent population (mean IQ of all maths A-level students) is then

      [102.75−1.96×1.537,102.75+1.96×1.537][102.75−1.96×1.537,102.75+1.96×1.537]

      ie

      [99.7,105.8][99.7,105.8]

      that is, we are 95% confident that the true population mean IQ lies between 99.7 and 105.8
